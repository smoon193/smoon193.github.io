assignment 1

Laser cut at least 30 instances of at least one unique part to form a press-fit construction kit. Must pass the shake test!


Sketching

For this assignment, I started out by sketching some ideas just to brainstorm what might be an interesting shape that could make a fun a construction kit. Maybe it was the beautiful cherry blossoms I've been seeing around, or maybe it was the springtime hay fever, but the first shape that came to mind was a flower. I kept sketching to try to challenge myself to come up with more complex and irregular shapes, shapes that would fit together in surprising ways, or shapes that fit a cute theme.
I tried to imagine what kinds of 3D forms you could make with a kit of each shape, especially if we could also incorporate other types of objects, like toothpicks or strings. I was in imminent danger of falling down a geometric rabbit hole when I realized I should probably just start cutting some stuff out and see what happened.


Prototyping

I got out an exacto knife, an old cutting mat from a brief foray into scrapbooking, and a sheet of foam and started cutting out some of the shapes I had sketched. I tried out an idea for an asterisk that could notch together to become a sort of 3D connector. I tried attaching some triangles and stars to that piece. I'm not sure what I was expecting, but I guess I was hoping I'd be able to make more of a closed shape?
I tested out some interlocking cats which were sorta fun, and some vaguely orange-inspired pieces, but I didn't like that those could really only fit together through one slot at the stem. I cut out a few flower shapes and tried different ways of fitting them together with the leaf pieces. At this point I started worrying about the limitations I would face trying to draw all these shapes in a software that was new to me.


Modeling

The next day I started trying to figure out the modeling part. I started in Rhino, but felt a little overwhelmed so I thought I could at least make a first draft in Cuttle. I thought the flower shape would be the easiest to get started with. I took a bunch of measurements of my cardboard piece with my calipers, but found that it varied quite a lot due to different squish coefficients (scientific term) in different spots of the cardboard. I took an average-ish of .1 inches, guessing that beyond that level of precision wouldn't be super valuable with this irregular of a material. I headed into the lab with a partially prepared Cuttle file, a few scraps of cardboard, and a dream.


Cutting & Refining

flo and I planned to try to catch each other at the lab, and I am so glad we did because my morale was much improved by having a friendly face to ask questions instead of flipping back and forth endlessly through the instructions, afraid to break something or accidentally commit arson. She walked me through my first cut. I set the speed to 25%, the power level to 100%, and this charming little fellow popped out!
But as cute as he may be, I tried to stick him onto the edge of my cardboard piece and he immediately fell off:-( For my second test cut, I narrowed the slots to .08 inches, and made the whole thing bigger just for fun.
Flower Test 2 successfully stayed on the edge of my cardboard square! I was a little concerned about things still fitting well as pieces got cut closer to the center, where the cardboard was less squished, but I figured it was better too snug than too loose since the material had some give. I tried cutting out a leaf shape next now that I had a workable slot size.

These fit together well enough, but I had grand ideas of being able to fit a leaf piece through the center hole of the flower, and then it could be secured on the other side of the hole by notching into the longer side slot of another leaf piece?? To what end... I don't know... Some sort of flower-wheeled jalopy?? Anyways... I tried one more flower cut with a bigger center hole, plus a leaf with shorter side slots.

Even with the bigger hole, my leaf/flower wheel wasn't working so hot. I also realized that the flowers would make terrible wheels. And that even if you did cobble together a two-wheeled contraption, it wouldn't be a very versatile piece because if you wanted it to have any chance of rolling you'd probably have to stop building with only five or so pieces, which isn't very fun. Oh well!! Maybe the next construction kit will be more functional than sculptural. In the meantime, I decided to go with my second flower iteration and my second leaf and cut 31 leaves and 18 flowers!

Once all the leaves and flowers had been cut, I tried assembling something with them. There were a few pieces that were a little dicey trying to notch together, but for the most part they fit great! I tried out building with as many different notch combinations as I could think of, and ended up with a sort of bush-looking sculpture. I gave it the Dairy Queen Blizzard test and it held together!
