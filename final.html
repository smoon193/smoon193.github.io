Gauge Measuring Square

I designed a gauge measuring square with a 4x4 inch window to easily measure your gauge, or how many stitches per inch you have, to ensure your final knitted product will fit how you want it to. 4x4 inches is a pretty standard gauge swatch, and having it as a little window vs. a floppy tape measure helps you press your fabric flat to get an accurate measurement.

I left a wider edge to give a cute Polaroid photo looking effect, and cut swirls into it so that it could also be used to hang a few of your favorite stitch markers when you're not using it.

Techniques used:
- Rhino modeling
- Laser cutting

Needle Holder

I had a bunch of tapestry and darning needles in my knitting kit, some loose and some in bulky packaging. I decided to make a more organized and space-efficient needle storage solution that could fit well into my existing kit!

I measured both types of needles and 3D printed some tests in the shape that I wanted the final needle holder to be. Once I had a had a decent fit, I 3D printed the negative of that shape to make a mold. The silicone is nice and bendy so it can flexibly fit into the nooks and crannies of my knitting kit!

Techniques used:
- Rhino modeling & mesh editing
- 3D printing
- Incorporating stock parts
- Mold design

Stitch Markers



I decided to make the practical, problem-solving stuff first, but I was most excited to try designing some stitch markers. These loop around your knitting needles to help you keep count of your stitches and separate different sections of your knitting.

I started by drawing some silly shapes like wiggly stars and hearts. Then I thought, how could I make these even more whimsical?? And engraved some text emojis into them. I added some jewelry hardware I had lying around to make a few different styles of stitch markers for all your stitch-marking needs!

Techniques used:
- Rhino modeling & mesh editing
- 3D printing
- Incorporating stock parts

Scissor Stopper

Look I know I said in my proposal that one of my extra time add-on projects would be needle stoppers not a scissor stopper but hear me out!! The mold ended up coming out with a shape that fit the flattened tip of my scissors better than the rounded tip of knitting needles, and I already have a few pairs of needle stoppers but nothing to protect the sharp end of my scissors!! Who am I to argue with fate!!

To stay on brand, I used one of the shapes I used to make the stitch markers, but extruded it a bit more and added a hole into the middle of the bottom instead of through it. Then I subtracted this shape from a rectangular prism to make a mold, which I 3D printed and then cast the silicone into.

Techniques used:
- Rhino modeling & mesh editing
- 3D printing
- Incorporating stock parts
- Mold design
